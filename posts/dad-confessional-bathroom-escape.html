Dad Confessional: The Bathroom Escape

March 9, 2026 · 9 min read

Let's get something out of the way: you have, at some point, locked yourself in the bathroom not because you needed to use it, but because you needed to not be touched, climbed on, screamed at, or asked for a snack for five consecutive minutes.

You're not alone. You're not a bad dad. You are a soldier retreating to the only room in the house with a lock on the door.

This is a safe space. These are real confessions from real dads. Names withheld to protect the guilty.

The Confessions

"The Phantom Poop"

"I told my wife I had to go to the bathroom. I didn't have to go. I sat on the closed toilet lid scrolling Reddit for 12 minutes. My two-year-old found me anyway. He slid his fingers under the door like a horror movie. I could see his little eyeball pressed against the gap. 'DADDY. DADDY. WHAT YOU DOING. YOU POOPING?' No, kid. I was trying to remember what silence sounds like."

— Dad of 2, ages 2 and 4

"The Strategic Work Call"

"I've started pretending I'm on work calls. I'll hold my phone to my ear and pace into the bedroom saying things like 'Absolutely, we need to sync on that deliverable.' There is no call. There is no deliverable. There is only me, walking in circles, having a conversation with no one because it buys me seven minutes of being left alone. My wife is suspicious. I work in construction."

— Dad of 3, ages 1, 3, and 5

"The Garage Guru"

"My garage isn't a workshop. I mean, there's a workbench in there. And tools. I bought a drill press last year. But I've never used any of it. The garage is just a room my kids aren't in. I go out there and sit in a camping chair and stare at the wall. Sometimes I eat a granola bar in total silence. It's the closest thing to meditation I've ever experienced."

— Dad of 2, ages 3 and 5

"The Grocery Run"

"My wife asked me to grab milk. I was gone for an hour and forty-five minutes. I went to three different stores. Not because any of them was out of milk. Because Trader Joe's has free samples and nobody at Trader Joe's calls me daddy. I also sat in the parking lot for twenty minutes listening to a podcast about the Napoleonic Wars. I learned a lot. I came home a better man."

— Dad of 1, age 18 months

"The Lock-In"

"I was in the bathroom — genuinely using it this time — and my three-year-old tried to open the door. Locked. She lay down on the floor and slid her entire arm under the door. Then a second arm. Then she started narrating: 'I'm coming in, Daddy. I'm coming to get you.' This child weighs 30 pounds and she was trying to commando-crawl under a door with a one-inch gap. She got stuck. I had to rescue her while still... mid-situation. Nobody tells you about this in the parenting books."

— Dad of 1, age 3

"The Driveway Decompression"

"Every day when I get home from work, I sit in the driveway for five to ten minutes before going inside. My wife knows. She can see my car through the window. She doesn't say anything because she does the same thing when she gets home first. We have an unspoken agreement that whoever arrives second gets the driveway time. It's the foundation of our marriage."

— Dad of 2, ages 2 and 4

"The Night Watch"

"I volunteer for every middle-of-the-night wakeup. My wife thinks I'm being selfless. The truth is, once the baby goes back to sleep, I have 20 to 30 minutes of pure, uninterrupted alone time in a dark, quiet house. No one needs anything from me. No one is talking. I sit on the couch and just... exist. It's the best part of my day and I feel guilty about that, but also I don't."

— Dad of 1, age 8 months

"The Double Agent"

"I learned that if I volunteer to do the grocery shopping AND take the toddler with me, my wife treats me like a hero. What she doesn't know is that Target has a Starbucks inside, and my son is perfectly content to sit in the cart eating free samples from the deli section while I drink a latte in peace. I'm not shopping. I'm at a cafe. With my son. Who is contained in a wheeled vehicle. It's the greatest hack I've ever discovered."

— Dad of 2, ages 1 and 3

Why We're Publishing This

Here's the thing nobody says out loud: needing five minutes alone doesn't mean you love your kids less. It means you're human.

The constant touching. The noise. The relentlessness of being needed every single second. It's a lot. It's beautiful and it's a lot.

A 2024 Ohio State University survey found that 66% of parents said the demands of parenthood "sometimes or frequently lead to isolation and loneliness." Sixty-two percent reported feeling burned out.

You know what those numbers really mean? They mean the dad sitting in his car in the Costco parking lot listening to a podcast is not having a crisis. He's managing one. Successfully.

So to every dad who's ever hidden in the bathroom, faked a work call, taken the scenic route home from daycare, or volunteered for a grocery run just to be alone with his thoughts for twenty minutes:

We see you. We are you.

And your secret is safe with us.
