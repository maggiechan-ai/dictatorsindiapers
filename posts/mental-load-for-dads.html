The Mental Load for Dads: It's Not Just a Mom Thing

March 23, 2026 · 12 min read

You know that comic that went viral a few years back? The one where a mom is standing in the kitchen, steam coming out of her ears, and the thought bubbles above her head are full of dentist appointments, grocery lists, school permission slips, birthday party RSVPs, and the realization that the dog needs his shots?

You looked at that comic and thought: Yeah, my wife goes through that.

You didn't think: Yeah, me too.

But here you are. It's 11:14 PM and you're lying in bed, wide awake, mentally scrolling through a list that no one assigned you and no one will ever see. Did you schedule that pediatrician appointment? The car registration expires next week. There's a slow leak in the bathroom faucet you keep meaning to fix. Your kid's shoes are too small — when did that happen? The daycare tuition auto-pay might have failed because you switched bank accounts. Oh, and your wife mentioned her mom's birthday is coming up, and you're pretty sure it's your job to order the gift because the last time she did it, you forgot to Venmo her.

Welcome to the mental load. Pull up a chair. You'll be here a while.

Wait, I Thought This Was a Mom Thing

Here's where the conversation usually goes sideways.

The "mental load" became a mainstream concept around 2017 thanks to a French comic called You Should've Asked by Emma. It nailed something millions of women had been feeling for years: the exhaustion of being the household project manager. The CEO of domesticity. The one who has to think about everything, not just do some of it when asked.

And the narrative — reasonably, honestly, accurately — centered on moms. Because statistically, mothers still carry the lion's share. A 2024 University of Bath study surveying 3,000 U.S. parents found that moms handle about 71% of household mental load tasks. Dads? Twenty-nine percent.

So this isn't a "but what about the dads" piece. This isn't an All Lives Matter riff on the mental load. Mothers carry more. Full stop.

But that 29%? It's not nothing. And for a lot of dads, it's growing. And nobody's talking about it, because dads aren't supposed to have a mental load. Dads are supposed to be the ones who need to be told to do things. The ones who "help" when asked. The bumbling sitcom husband who can't find the diapers even though they're in the same spot they've been for nine months.

If that's not you — if you're running logistics in your head at 2 AM, if you're managing finances, fixing the house, planning the weekends, tracking your kid's developmental milestones on your phone while pretending to watch SportsCenter — then congratulations. You've got the mental load. And you probably don't know what to call it.

The Dad Version of the Mental Load

Here's the thing nobody tells you: the dad mental load often looks different from the mom mental load, which is part of why it's invisible.

Mothers tend to carry more of the daily, repetitive cognitive labor — the Bath study found they handle about 79% of recurring tasks like childcare logistics and cleaning schedules. Dads, meanwhile, tend to cluster around what researchers call "episodic" tasks: finances, home maintenance, insurance, car stuff, long-term planning. The things that don't need doing every day but never fully leave your brain.

Sound familiar? Here's what it looks like on any given Tuesday:

  • The furnace filter needs replacing. You've been meaning to do it for three weeks. Every time you walk past the utility closet, a small voice in your head says filter.
  • Your homeowner's insurance renewal is coming up and you're pretty sure you're overpaying but comparing plans requires approximately four hours of your life that don't exist.
  • Your kid's bike has a wobbly wheel. Not dangerous yet. Probably. You think about it every time they ride it.
  • The family budget is tight this month because of two unexpected expenses, and you're quietly shuffling money between accounts without mentioning it because you don't want to stress your partner out.
  • Your father-in-law's birthday is in eight days. Nobody has discussed a plan. You suspect you are the plan.

None of that is on a shared to-do list. None of it is visible. None of it gets a thank-you. It just lives in your head, taking up RAM, running in the background like forty browser tabs you can't close.

The Invisible Weight of Being the "Steady One"

Here's a dimension of the dad mental load that's uniquely brutal: the emotional labor of performing stability.

A lot of dads carry an unspoken mandate to be the calm one. The steady hand. The person who doesn't freak out when the car makes a weird noise or the kid falls off the playground structure or the bank account dips below the comfort zone. Not because anyone explicitly said "you're not allowed to have feelings," but because somewhere between your own dad's stoicism and every action movie you ever watched, you absorbed the idea that your job is to hold it together.

So you hold it together. You carry the financial anxiety silently. You worry about your kid's speech delay but don't bring it up because your wife is already worried and you don't want to pile on. You mentally calculate whether you can afford the vacation she's excited about while smiling and saying "yeah, let's do it."

A 2024 Parenting Across Scotland survey found that 48% of dads reported their mental health as "not great" or "very poor" — more than double the rate from the previous year. And here's the gut punch: the number of dads not seeking support for declining mental health more than doubled too, from 6% in 2022 to 15% in 2024.

We're carrying more. We're telling nobody. This is not a great strategy.

The Perception Gap (aka "I Do More Than You Think")

Let's talk about the most common fight in your house. No, not the one about loading the dishwasher wrong. (Although, seriously, there IS a correct way and it involves facing the dirty side toward the center. I will die on this hill.)

The fight about who does more.

Research calls this the "perception gap." Both parents genuinely believe they're carrying the heavier load. Both are, in some sense, right — because they're each carrying a load the other doesn't fully see.

Your partner sees that she remembered picture day, scheduled the dentist, bought the new socks, and arranged the playdate. She doesn't see that you spent forty-five minutes on the phone with your insurance provider, quietly moved $800 from savings to cover the car repair, and spent your lunch break researching preschools because the application deadlines are next month.

You see all the stuff you do. She sees all the stuff she does. Neither of you sees the full picture. And instead of comparing notes, you end up keeping score, which is about as productive as negotiating with a toddler about bedtime.

The solution isn't a spreadsheet. (Okay, it might be a spreadsheet. Some of us are like that.) The solution is visibility. Actually talking about the invisible stuff. Saying "hey, here's what's been on my mind" instead of silently carrying it like some kind of suburban martyr.

Why Dads Don't Talk About This

Quick survey of reasons dads don't bring up the mental load:

  1. "It sounds like complaining." You're a dad. You signed up for this. Talking about how hard it is feels like asking for a medal for doing your job.
  2. "My partner has it harder." And she probably does, in certain categories. So bringing up your own load feels like one-upping her, which is the opposite of helpful.
  3. "Real men just handle it." The voice of every culture, every movie, every locker room conversation you've ever had. Dads provide. Dads fix. Dads don't whine about the cognitive load of providing and fixing.
  4. "Nobody cares." This one stings because it sometimes feels true. The cultural conversation about parental mental load is overwhelmingly (and understandably) focused on moms. There's no comic for dads lying awake worrying about the retirement account.
  5. "I don't even have language for it." Before the term "mental load" entered the vocabulary, how would you even describe this? "I'm tired but not from doing things, from thinking about things"? That sounds insane. But it's real.

The Financial Load: The Weight Nobody Talks About

Let's park here for a second, because this is a big one.

For a lot of dads, especially in families where traditional roles still have gravity, the financial mental load is enormous. And it's almost entirely invisible.

You're tracking the mortgage payment, the daycare cost (which, by the way, averages over $16,000 a year in the U.S. and more than college in many states), the car payment, the insurance premiums, the emergency fund that's not quite where it should be, the student loans that won't die, and the vague but persistent awareness that you should be saving more for retirement and also starting a 529 plan.

A 2025 Insure.com Father's Day Index estimated that the average dad performs nearly $61,000 worth of unpaid labor annually. Still less than half of what's attributed to moms ($145,000+), but not exactly pocket change in terms of cognitive effort.

And here's the thing about financial stress: it's constant. It doesn't clock out. It doesn't take weekends. It follows you into the shower and sits with you at dinner and whispers in your ear at 3 AM. And because money is one of those topics many couples struggle to discuss openly, a lot of dads carry it alone.

"Sandwich Dads" and the Double Mental Load

If you're in your mid-thirties to early forties, there's a decent chance you're dealing with what researchers call the "sandwich" effect: caring for your young kids while also beginning to navigate aging parents.

Your dad needs a new specialist. Your mom keeps forgetting things. There's a conversation about assisted living that nobody wants to have. And all of this is layered on top of the normal chaos of raising a tiny dictator who just flushed a toy car down the toilet.

This is the double mental load, and it's brutal. Because it's not just logistics — it's grief. You're watching your parents age while simultaneously raising the next generation, and the emotional weight of that temporal sandwich is something nobody prepares you for.

So What Do You Do About It?

Look, I'm not going to wrap this up with a neat bow and five actionable tips that'll fix everything. That would be dishonest. But here's what seems to help, based on actual research and actual dads who've been in these trenches:

Name It

Seriously. Just knowing the term "mental load" and being able to say "hey, I'm carrying a lot of mental load right now" to your partner is a game-changer. It gives language to the invisible. It takes the thing from a vague, shapeless anxiety to something you can actually discuss.

Make the Invisible Visible

The shared calendar. The whiteboard in the kitchen. The running list in the Notes app that both of you can access. Whatever works. The point is that both partners can see what both partners are carrying. When the labor is visible, it's easier to divide fairly.

Own Domains, Don't Split Tasks

Instead of trying to divide every task 50/50, take full ownership of entire domains. You own finances. She owns medical. You own weekend activities. She owns school logistics. Whatever makes sense. When you own a domain, you own the thinking, not just the doing. That's the difference between carrying the mental load and just following instructions.

Talk About the Money Stuff

If financial stress is eating you alive and you're not talking about it, start. It doesn't have to be a full spreadsheet presentation. Even "hey, I've been stressed about money lately, can we look at our budget together?" is a start. Carrying financial anxiety alone is not sustainable, no matter how many generations of men before you modeled exactly that behavior.

Go to Therapy (Yeah, I Said It)

Only 15% of dads in that Scottish survey weren't seeking support for worsening mental health. That number should be zero. Talking to a professional about the weight you're carrying isn't weakness. It's maintenance. You'd take the car to a mechanic. You'd call a plumber for the sink. Your brain deserves the same attention.

Let Go of Some Tabs

Not everything in your head needs to be there. Some of those mental browser tabs can be closed. The furnace filter? Set a recurring reminder in your phone and stop thinking about it. The insurance comparison? Block out an hour this Saturday and then it's done. Part of managing the mental load is recognizing that not everything deserves permanent mental real estate.

The Part Where I Get Real

Here's the truth that sits underneath all of this: the mental load isn't a competition. It's not about who carries more. It's about the fact that raising kids in the modern world requires an absolutely bonkers amount of cognitive labor, and most families are trying to do it with two exhausted people and no instruction manual.

Moms carry more, statistically. That's real and that matters and we should keep working to close that gap.

But dads carrying their own invisible weight in silence isn't helping anyone. Not you. Not your partner. Not your kids. The regime runs better when both co-commanders can be honest about what's on their plate.

So the next time you're lying in bed at 11 PM, running through the mental checklist — the car, the bills, the leaky faucet, the school thing, the birthday, the retirement account, the kid's weird rash that's probably nothing but what if it's not — know that you're not alone. This is real. It counts. And it's okay to say it out loud.

Even dictators need a debrief sometimes.
