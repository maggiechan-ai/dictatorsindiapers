Welcome to the Regime: A Manifesto for Dads Who Are Barely Holding It Together

March 7, 2026 · 10 min read

You're standing in your kitchen at 5:43 AM. Not because you wanted to be. Not because you're a "morning person." Because a three-foot-tall dictator with no pants and an iron will decided that today starts now. RIGHT now. And they've brought demands.

Milk. But not that milk. The other milk. In the blue cup. No, the OTHER blue cup. The one that may or may not exist anymore because it might be under the couch, or in the car, or in a dimension your toddler can access but you cannot.

Welcome to the regime.

What This Is

This is Dictators in Diapers. And if you're here, you already know what it means.

It means you've been headbutted during a goodnight kiss. It means you've eaten a cold dinner standing over the sink at 9 PM because sitting down would wake the baby. It means you've Googled "is it normal for a toddler to scream for 45 minutes about socks" and felt genuine relief when the answer was yes.

This isn't a parenting blog. Not really. We don't have "10 Tips for a Stress-Free Morning Routine" because that's not a real thing that exists for anyone with a child under five. We're not going to tell you to "cherish every moment" because some moments are terrible and the people who say that are either lying or have forgotten.

This is a place for dads. Specifically, dads of kids zero to five — the years where you're simultaneously the most important person in someone's life and also just a large, warm surface to wipe things on.

Why Dads

Here's the thing nobody talks about at the baby shower.

Dads are more involved than ever. We're changing more diapers, doing more nighttime feeds, spending more time — about 75 minutes a day on childcare now, up from 25 minutes a day in the mid-1960s. Ninety-seven percent of us are changing diapers. The number of stay-at-home dads jumped 70% between 1989 and 2014. We're HERE. We're showing up. Eighty-five percent of fathers say being a parent is one of the most important parts of who they are.

And yet.

The pediatrician talks to your wife. The parenting books are written for moms. The baby-changing table is in the women's restroom. The playgroup assumes you're babysitting. The world built an entire infrastructure of support for new parents and then pointed a big arrow that says "MOMS ONLY."

One in five dads loses close friends within a year of their kid being born. A quarter of new fathers report feeling isolated. Seventy percent say their stress levels jumped in that first year. One in ten will experience depression or anxiety during their partner's pregnancy or the first year after. And almost none of them will talk about it. Because we were raised in a world that told us to tough it out, walk it off, provide and shut up.

That's not good enough. Not for us, and not for the tiny humans depending on us.

What We're Not

We're not a parenting advice column. We are spectacularly unqualified for that.

We're not a "dad influencer" thing. Nobody here is going to tell you about their morning routine or try to sell you a course on "intentional fatherhood." If someone uses the word "intentional" more than once, they've been compromised. Abort.

We're not anti-mom. We love moms. Moms are incredible. Our partners are doing this same impossible thing, usually while also recovering from the physical act of producing an entire human being with their body. This isn't about taking anything away from them. It's about building something for us.

We're not a pity party, either. Yeah, this is hard. It's also the best thing most of us have ever done. Those two things coexist. That's kind of the whole point.

What We Actually Are

We're dispatches from the front lines.

We're the 2 AM text to your buddy who also has a newborn: "Dude, mine just projectile vomited across the room like a fire hose. New record."

We're the honest conversation at the bar after the kids are finally asleep, where one guy admits he cried in the garage last Tuesday and the other guy says "Only Tuesday?"

We're the stuff you wish someone had told you before the baby came. Not the soft-focus, beautiful-journey version. The real version. The one with bodily fluids and identity crises and a love so intense it scares you sometimes.

This is a community built on three things:

Honesty. The unfiltered, sometimes-ugly truth about what it's like to raise tiny humans. No performance. No "blessed" captions. Just the real thing.

Humor. Because laughing about the chaos is how you survive it. Your kid threw a full bowl of oatmeal at the dog and then cried because the dog had oatmeal on it? That's comedy. That's a dispatch from the front lines. That's the kind of story that makes another dad at 3 AM go, "Oh thank god, it's not just me."

Heart. Under all the jokes, there's something real here. You're reading this because you give a damn. Because you want to be a good dad, whatever that means. Because even on the worst days — the screaming, the mess, the bone-deep exhaustion — you wouldn't trade this for anything. And on the days you WOULD trade it? That's okay too. We don't judge. We just pass the whiskey.

The Regime Metaphor (Because We Commit to Our Bits)

Your child is a dictator. This isn't an insult. It's a factual observation.

They have absolute power over your schedule, your sleep, your body, your emotional state, and your Netflix queue. They make demands without negotiation. They change the rules without warning. They can go from laughing to screaming to laughing again in under eleven seconds. They have no concept of compromise, fairness, or the fact that you also have needs.

They have seized control of the household and they will not be giving it back.

You are not a parent. You are a citizen of the regime. You live under their rule. Your job is survival, service, and — when possible — smuggling in some joy between the tantrums.

We call our blog posts "dispatches." Our email is "classified intel." Our community is "the regime." Because calling it what it is — absolute, joyful, terrifying chaos — is more honest than pretending we've got it under control.

Nobody has it under control. Some of us are just better at hiding it.

Who This Is For

This is for the dad who just came home from the hospital and is currently staring at a newborn like it's an alien artifact he's supposed to keep alive. (You will. Probably. They're surprisingly durable.)

This is for the dad of a toddler who just spent forty-five minutes negotiating about whether pants are mandatory. (The answer, legally, is yes. Good luck enforcing it.)

This is for the dad who's back at work pretending to care about Q2 projections while his brain is just a loop of "is the baby breathing? check the monitor. is the baby breathing? check the monitor."

This is for the dad who feels lonely but won't say it. Who feels overwhelmed but thinks he shouldn't. Who feels like he's failing but won't ask for help because — what even IS the help? Where's the manual? There's no manual. There was never a manual.

This is for the dad who loves his kids so much it physically hurts sometimes, and also just wants to watch one entire episode of something without being interrupted.

You're not alone. One in four of us feels isolated. But there are 72 million fathers in the United States alone, spending more time with their kids than any generation before them. You are part of something massive and mostly invisible, and we're here to make it a little less invisible.

What's Coming

Dispatches. Lots of them.

Field guides to tantrums, tactical breakdowns of bedtime, honest essays about the identity crisis that nobody warns you about. Confessionals from anonymous dads — the bathroom-escape stories, the moments of doubt, the weird things your kid did that you can't tell anyone about in polite company.

We'll talk about the fun stuff: ranking kids' TV shows by adult watchability, video games you can play with a toddler on your lap, the definitive diaper bag loadout.

We'll talk about the hard stuff: postpartum depression in dads (yes, it's real — 10% of new fathers experience it), dad rage, loneliness, what happens to your relationship when you're both too tired to finish a sentence.

We'll talk about the stuff in between: the first time you made your kid laugh so hard they fell over, the moment you realized you'd become your own father, the time your toddler said "I love you daddy" completely unprompted and you had to leave the room so they wouldn't see you cry.

All of it. The whole messy, magnificent, exhausting, hilarious thing.

Join the Regime

Here's the deal. You showed up. That already makes you the kind of dad we want here.

If anything in this manifesto made you nod, or laugh, or feel slightly less insane — you're one of us. You've been one of us since the first time a tiny human screamed at you for giving them exactly what they asked for.

So subscribe. Follow along. Send us your stories. Tell us about your worst Tuesday. Tell us about the time your kid did something so bizarre you had to sit on the floor and reevaluate your entire life.

We're building this thing together. The regime may never sleep, but at least we can be awake together.
