Therapy for Dads: Why You Should Go and How to Start

March 28, 2026 · 10 min read

You're sitting in the car after drop-off. The engine's running. You're not moving. There's no reason to sit here except that you don't know where to put what you're feeling, and you've been like this for — how long now? Weeks? Months?

You won't call it depression because it doesn't look like the commercials. You're not staring out a rain-streaked window. You're functioning. You go to work. You make dinner. You do the bath-book-bed thing. But something's off. Something's been off. And the thought that keeps circling — the one you won't say out loud — is: I think I need to talk to someone.

This dispatch is for that guy. The one in the car. The one who's been fine for a suspicious amount of time.

The Numbers Don't Lie (Even If You Do)

Here's the thing about dads and mental health: we're drowning in data that says we're not okay, and drowning in silence that says we should be.

1 in 10 fathers experience depression or anxiety during their partner's pregnancy or in the first year after birth. Some research puts it higher — up to 24% of new dads in the postnatal period. Not a fringe stat. Not a "sensitive guys" thing. Nearly a quarter.

Anxiety is even worse. Up to 18% of new fathers experience clinically significant generalized anxiety, OCD, or PTSD in baby's first year. One study found that up to 51% of dads experience anxiety disorders in the postnatal period. Fifty-one percent. That's not a minority. That's most of us.

And here's the kicker: it often doesn't show up when you'd expect. A 2026 study found that while dads may seem fine during pregnancy and right after birth, diagnoses for depression and stress-related disorders spike about a year later. Peak onset? Three to six months postpartum. Right when everyone thinks you should be "settled in."

Over 6 million men in the U.S. experience depression annually. Men are four times more likely to die by suicide than women. And yet — only 7.2% of men engage in talk therapy, compared to 11.7% of women. Only 13.4% of men receive any mental health treatment at all.

We're not fine. We're just quiet about it.

Why You're Not Going (And Why Those Reasons Are Garbage)

Look, I get it. I'm not going to sit here and pretend the barriers are imaginary. They're real. But most of them are stories we tell ourselves — stories we inherited from men who never had permission to feel anything.

"I don't need therapy. I'm fine."

This is the big one. In surveys, 55% of men who haven't sought therapy say it's because they don't think they need it. And I believe them. They genuinely believe they're fine.

But here's what "fine" looks like for a lot of dads: shorter temper. Less patience. Checking out mentally. A couple more beers than usual. Scrolling your phone at 1 AM not because you can't sleep but because you don't want to be alone with your thoughts. Going through the motions at work, at home, everywhere.

That's not fine. That's coping. And coping has an expiration date.

Depression in men doesn't always look like sadness. It looks like irritability, anger, sudden outbursts, withdrawal, low motivation, reckless behavior, increased substance use. The stuff that gets celebrated as "blowing off steam" or dismissed as "just being stressed."

If someone asked your partner — really asked — they'd probably say they noticed something months ago.

"Therapy is for people with real problems."

Your kid is relying on you for emotional regulation they literally cannot do themselves. Your partner is depending on you to be present. You're navigating sleep deprivation, identity shifts, relationship strain, financial pressure, and a society that barely acknowledges dads exist in the parenting equation.

Those are real problems. The idea that you need to hit rock bottom before you earn a therapist's time is like saying you shouldn't see a doctor until the bone is actually sticking out.

"I can handle it on my own."

Maybe. For now. But research on paternal mental health shows that when dads are struggling, it radiates outward. Paternal depression is linked to less attention to baby's health, higher risk of behavioral problems in preschool-age kids, and poorer child development across social, emotional, cognitive, and language domains. Effects that can last into adolescence.

You're not just carrying this for yourself. Handling it on your own isn't stoic. It's risky.

"I wouldn't even know what to say."

Nobody does. That's literally what the therapist is for. You don't need to arrive with a TED Talk. You can walk in and say "I don't know why I'm here, but something feels wrong." That's enough. That's more than enough.

"It costs too much."

Fair. Therapy isn't cheap — 32% of dads cite cost as a barrier. But here's the practical reality: most insurance plans cover mental health visits. Your employer might offer an EAP (Employee Assistance Program) with free sessions — usually 3 to 8. Online therapy platforms run $60–$100 per session. Community health centers offer sliding-scale fees. Some therapists offer reduced rates if you ask.

It's not free. But neither is the alternative.

What Actually Happens in Therapy (Spoiler: No One Makes You Lie on a Couch)

If your image of therapy comes from movies — dim room, leather couch, guy with glasses nodding while you talk about your mother — update the firmware.

Modern therapy, especially for men, tends to be structured, goal-oriented, and practical. You're not paying someone to listen to you ramble. You're working with a trained professional to:

  • Identify patterns you can't see yourself
  • Build strategies for handling stress, anger, or anxiety
  • Process stuff you've been carrying — from childhood, from the transition to fatherhood, from last Tuesday
  • Develop actual skills for emotional regulation (which, by the way, your toddler desperately needs you to have)

A few types worth knowing about:

CBT (Cognitive Behavioral Therapy): The most common and well-researched. Focuses on identifying thought patterns that are screwing you up and replacing them with ones that don't. Structured, often with "homework." Feels productive.

Solution-Focused Therapy: Less "tell me about your childhood," more "what do you want to change and how do we get there." Good for dads who want action items.

Internal Family Systems (IFS): You have different "parts" of yourself — the protector, the manager, the exile. This approach helps you understand which part is running the show and why. Sounds weird. Works surprisingly well for men.

Group Therapy / Dad Support Groups: Postpartum Support International runs dad-specific groups. There's something about hearing another dad say the thing you've been thinking that no amount of individual therapy can replicate.

How to Actually Start (The Practical Part)

You've read this far. You're at least curious. Here's the step-by-step, because "just find a therapist" is about as helpful as "just sleep when the baby sleeps."

Step 1: Figure Out What You're Dealing With

You don't need a diagnosis. Just a direction. Are you angry all the time? Anxious? Numb? Having trouble connecting with your kid? Fighting with your partner more? Can't sleep or sleeping too much? Write it down if it helps. Even a bullet list on your phone.

Step 2: Check Your Insurance

Call the number on the back of your card and say: "I want to find a therapist in-network for individual therapy." They'll send you a list. It won't be fun. It will be worth it.

If you don't have insurance or your coverage sucks:

  • Ask your employer about an EAP (free, confidential, usually 3–8 sessions)
  • Check Open Path Collective — sessions run $30–$80
  • Look into community mental health centers in your area
  • Online platforms like BetterHelp or Cerebral offer sliding-scale options

Step 3: Make a Short List

Use Psychology Today's therapist finder (psychologytoday.com/us/therapists). Filter by your insurance, location, and issues. Look for therapists who list men's issues, fatherhood, life transitions, or anger management.

Only about 20% of therapists are male. If having a male therapist matters to you, filter for it. If it doesn't, don't. What matters is the connection, not the gender.

Step 4: Make the Call (Or Send the Email)

Most therapists offer a free 15-minute consultation. Use it. Ask:

  • "Have you worked with dads or new fathers before?"
  • "What's your approach?"
  • "What does a typical session look like?"

You're interviewing them. If the vibe is off, move on. Finding the right therapist is like finding the right mechanic — the first one might not be it.

Step 5: Show Up

That's it. You don't need to prepare a monologue. You don't need to have your feelings organized. You just need to be in the room (or on the screen — telehealth counts).

The hardest part is the first session. After that, it's just a thing you do. Like the gym, except for your brain, and nobody makes you do burpees.

The Stuff Nobody Tells You About Being a Dad in Therapy

It might feel weird at first. Especially if you've spent 30+ years handling everything internally. Talking about feelings to a stranger is awkward. That's normal. Awkward is not the same as wrong.

Your partner will notice before you do. The changes, I mean. Less snapping. More patience. Actually being present instead of just being in the room. They'll notice.

It's not a sign you've failed. When dads who are in therapy were asked who they confide in most, 77% said their therapist — more than their spouse, friends, or family. That's not failure. That's having the right tool for the job.

You're modeling something for your kids. Your son or daughter is going to grow up and face hard things. The question is whether they'll have a template for asking for help or a template for suffering in silence. You're building that template right now.

It doesn't have to be forever. Some guys go for six sessions and get what they need. Some go for years. There's no minimum and no expectation. You go until you don't need to.

Resources That Actually Help

  • Postpartum Support International (PSI): Free online dad support groups, monthly chats, and a helpline at 1-800-944-4773 (call or text)
  • Man Therapy (mantherapy.org): Evidence-based resource built specifically for men. Includes a self-assessment.
  • PostpartumMen.com: Self-assessment and forum for dads dealing with mood issues after baby
  • Psychology Today Therapist Finder: psychologytoday.com/us/therapists
  • Crisis Text Line: Text HOME to 741741
  • 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline: Call or text 988

The Bottom Line

You wouldn't run the engine on empty because you're "probably fine." You wouldn't ignore a check engine light for six months because you're "handling it."

You're a dad. You signed up for the hardest, most important job that exists. The regime needs you operational — not just surviving, but actually present. Actually connected. Actually okay.

The car is still running. Drop-off is over. You've got an hour before the next thing.

Make the call.
