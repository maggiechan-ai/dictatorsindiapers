Potty Training: A War Story in Three Acts

March 14, 2026 · 10 min read

It's 7:14 AM on a Saturday. You're standing in your bathroom holding a tiny plastic throne covered in cartoon dinosaurs. Your two-year-old is standing across from you, pants around her ankles, staring at the potty like you just asked her to defuse a bomb.

She looks at the potty. She looks at you. She looks at the potty again.

Then she turns around, squats behind the couch, and handles her business on the carpet like a feral cat asserting dominance.

Welcome to potty training. The longest, dumbest, most emotionally devastating campaign you will ever wage against a person who weighs thirty pounds.

ACT I: The Propaganda Phase (a.k.a. "We're Gonna Do This!")

Every war begins with optimism. You've read the blogs. You've watched the YouTube videos. You bought the book — the one your wife's Facebook group swears by — and you skimmed the first three chapters while sitting on the toilet yourself, which feels poetically appropriate.

You've got the gear. The little potty. The big-toilet seat insert. The step stool. The training underwear with trucks on them. The sticker chart. The M&Ms for rewards. You're basically running a military supply chain for a person whose primary life skill is putting raisins in their nose.

Your partner looks at you with a mix of hope and pity. "You sure you're ready for this?"

"Born ready," you say, like an absolute fool.

Here's what nobody tells you about potty training: the timeline has shifted dramatically. Back in the 1950s, most kids were trained by 28 months. Today? A 2025 Toilet Training Census found that only 61% of kids are potty trained by age two and a half — down from 95% in 2004. The average age has crept up to around 37 months. So if your kid is pushing three and still in diapers, you're not behind. You're statistically normal. Which is comforting and also horrifying.

The American Academy of Pediatrics says most kids are developmentally ready somewhere between 18 and 36 months. That's an 18-month window. Eighteen months! That's not a window, that's a wall you're staring at.

But you don't care about averages. You care about YOUR kid. And YOUR kid just told the potty "no thank you" with the calm authority of a diplomat refusing a trade deal.

The Signs of "Readiness" (According to Experts Who Don't Live With Your Child)

Every pediatrician will tell you to look for "signs of readiness." These include:

  • Staying dry for two hours at a stretch
  • Showing interest in the toilet
  • Telling you when their diaper is wet or dirty
  • Being able to pull pants up and down
  • Wanting to be independent

Your child displays exactly one of these: the independence one. Except they apply it exclusively to refusing everything you suggest.

You begin anyway.

Day one. You set a timer for every twenty minutes. Every twenty minutes, you cheerfully ask, "Do you need to use the potty?"

Every twenty minutes, the answer is no.

At the thirty-seven-minute mark, your kid pees on the kitchen floor while making direct eye contact with you. Not defiant eye contact. Curious eye contact. Like a scientist observing your reaction.

You smile. You stay calm. You say, "That's okay! Accidents happen! Let's try the potty next time!"

Inside, you are broken.

ACT II: The Siege (a.k.a. "Please, For the Love of God, Just Sit on It")

Day three, and your house smells like a frat party during rush week. You've gone through more paper towels than a Bounty commercial. Your kid has peed on the floor, the rug, the couch, in their shoe, and — in a moment of impressive creativity — on the dog.

The dog looked at you. You looked at the dog. Neither of you signed up for this.

Here's the thing about potty training that the cheerful Instagram parents won't show you: it gets worse before it gets better. The University of Michigan's Mott Children's Hospital poll found that 20% of parents reported their child experienced potty anxiety, and another 20% found the process harder than they expected. Which means 40% of parents are out there white-knuckling it just like you.

And dads? We get a special bonus level. Because a lot of potty training advice is written for moms. The social infrastructure of it — the Facebook groups, the mommy blogs, the pediatrician conversations — it all sort of assumes dad is somewhere in the background, maybe holding a towel. But you're in the room, man. You're on your knees next to a tiny toilet at 6 AM, singing a song about going pee-pee to the tune of "Old MacDonald," and wondering what happened to the guy who used to play guitar in a band.

The Negotiation Phase

Around day five, you attempt diplomacy.

"If you use the potty, you get an M&M."

Your kid considers this. You can see the wheels turning. They're calculating the ROI on this deal like a tiny Warren Buffett in a Pull-Up.

"Two M&Ms," they counter.

"...Fine."

They sit on the potty. Nothing happens. They sit there for fourteen minutes while you read The Very Hungry Caterpillar six times. Then they stand up, pull up their training pants, walk three feet away, and immediately pee.

You give them the M&Ms anyway because at this point you've lost all leverage and you both know it.

The Regression

Just when you think you're making progress — a few successful trips, some dry mornings, a general sense that maybe civilization is possible — your kid regresses.

Regression. That's the clinical term for "your kid knew how to do this yesterday and has decided, as a power move, to forget."

Pediatricians say regression is normal and usually triggered by stress — a new sibling, a schedule change, a new daycare, or sometimes just the sheer toddler whim of deciding that yesterday's rules no longer apply because they are, after all, a dictator.

The Cleveland Clinic advises staying calm, going back to basics, and consulting your pediatrician if it lasts more than a month. Research shows that chronic constipation is a frequently overlooked culprit — a lot of kids who seem to be "refusing" are actually uncomfortable, which makes sense when you think about it. Hard to be cooperative when your plumbing's backed up.

But knowing this doesn't help at 3 PM on a Tuesday when your kid looks you dead in the eye and says "I don't DO potty anymore" like they're a retired athlete walking away from the sport.

ACT III: The Surrender (That's Actually a Victory)

Here's the plot twist they don't put in the books: you don't win potty training. You survive it.

One day — and you won't see it coming — your kid will just... do it. They'll walk to the bathroom, pull down their pants, sit on the potty, and go. Like they've been doing it their whole life. Like the last three weeks of psychological warfare never happened.

You'll be standing in the hallway, holding a package of Pull-Ups you just bought in bulk, staring at a child who has apparently decided, on their own timeline and nobody else's, that they're done with diapers.

And you'll feel... everything. Relief. Pride. Exhaustion. A creeping suspicion that they could have done this the whole time and were just messing with you.

(They were. They absolutely were.)

The Stuff That Actually Helped

Look, I'm not a pediatrician and I don't play one on the internet. But here's what worked in the trenches:

Let them watch you. Yeah, it's weird. But kids learn by imitation, and nothing demystifies the toilet like seeing Dad use it. You will feel like you're performing a one-man show for a very unimpressed audience. Do it anyway.

Ditch the diapers cold turkey. Some dads swear by the rip-the-band-aid approach. No more diapers during the day. Training pants or underwear only. Yes, there will be casualties. Accept the carpet as a sacrifice to the cause.

Don't punish accidents. This one's real. The AAP is clear: punishment backfires. It creates anxiety, hiding, and holding — which leads to constipation, which leads to more problems. Keep it neutral. "Oops, let's clean up and try the potty next time." Save your frustration for after bedtime, when you can scream into a pillow like the rest of us.

Watch for the constipation thing. Seriously. If your kid is resisting the potty hard, refusing to poop, or having painful bowel movements, talk to your pediatrician. Research shows this is one of the most common — and most overlooked — causes of potty training failure. It's not stubbornness. It's discomfort.

Respect their timeline. Remember that stat — 61% trained by two and a half, which means 39% are NOT. Your kid isn't broken. Their brain and their bladder are on their own schedule. Pushing too hard before they're ready doesn't speed things up. It usually slows things down.

Celebrate like it's the Super Bowl. When they DO go on the potty? Lose your mind. Cheer. High-five. Do a victory dance. Call grandma. Make it the biggest deal in the history of deals. Positive reinforcement works better than anything else, and your kid's face when you're genuinely proud of them? That's the good stuff, man.

A Note for the Dads Doing This Solo

If you're a single dad potty training, or your partner's back at work and you're running this op alone — first of all, respect. Second, 22% of parents in recent surveys started potty training at age three, not two. You're not late. You're not failing. You're doing one of the most annoying, patience-destroying tasks in all of parenting, and you're doing it without backup.

The fact that you're reading an article about it means you give a damn. That already puts you ahead.

The After-Action Report

Potty training is the great equalizer of parenthood. Doesn't matter if you're a CEO or a plumber, a first-time dad or a dad of four. At some point, you will be crouched on a bathroom floor, bribing a small person with chocolate, negotiating with someone who has zero concept of consequences, and questioning every life choice that led to this moment.

And then it's over. And weirdly, you miss the simplicity of diapers. For about five seconds. Before you realize you never have to buy another box of Pampers again and a wave of financial relief washes over you like a warm bath you haven't been able to take in two years.

The regime will test you. Potty training is just one campaign in a longer war. But you'll get through it. Your kid will get through it. And someday, years from now, you'll tell this story at a dinner party, and your kid — now a mortified teenager — will beg you to stop.

Don't stop.

It's the best war story you've got. 🫡
