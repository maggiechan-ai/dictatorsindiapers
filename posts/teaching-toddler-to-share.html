Teaching a Toddler to Share (When You Yourself Don't Want To)

March 17, 2026 · 10 min read

Let's start with a confession.

Last Tuesday, my two-year-old grabbed a handful of my fries. Not her fries. My fries. The ones I specifically ordered for myself because she said she didn't want any. The ones I was savoring in the exact three-minute window between her finishing her chicken nuggets and deciding she was bored.

She looked me dead in the eyes. Shoved three fries in her mouth at once. Didn't blink.

And my first thought — my honest, unfiltered, adult-man-with-a-mortgage first thought — was: "That's mine."

So yeah. I'm supposed to be teaching this kid to share. Me. The guy who angles his phone screen away when his wife walks by so she doesn't see how much DoorDash he ordered. The guy who eats the good snacks after bedtime like a raccoon going through a dumpster. The guy who once told his buddy "I only have one beer left" when he had four.

I am the sharing teacher. God help us all.

The "Mine" Phase Is Not a Phase. It's a Lifestyle.

Here's the thing nobody tells you: when your toddler screams "MINE!" at the playground, they're not being a monster. They're being developmentally appropriate.

According to researchers at the National Institutes of Health, genuine sharing behavior — the kind where a kid voluntarily gives up something they value to benefit someone else — doesn't really kick in until around age 3.5 to 4. Before that, the concept of ownership is still forming. Kids start using possessive words like "mine" late in their second year, and for a while, that word is basically their entire constitutional framework.

Think about it. Your two-year-old has recently discovered that things can belong to them. That's huge. That's like a caveman discovering fire, except the fire is a sticky plastic dinosaur and the caveman will wage total war if you try to take it.

Developmental psychologists call this "ownership understanding," and it's actually positively associated with sharing later on. The kid who screams "MINE!" at two is building the cognitive foundation to understand what it means to give something away at four. It's infrastructure. Ugly, screaming, snot-covered infrastructure.

The Playground Tribunal

You know where the sharing crisis really detonates? The playground.

Picture this: Your kid is on the swing. Another kid walks up and wants a turn. Your kid does what any reasonable tiny dictator would do — grips the chains tighter, leans back, and pretends the other child doesn't exist.

Now you're standing there, and the other parent is standing there, and you're both doing the thing where you smile too widely and say helpful things like, "Okay, buddy, how about we let Aiden have a turn?"

Your kid responds by going completely boneless.

And somewhere in the back of your brain, a voice whispers: I mean, we were here first.

Because here's the dirty secret of the sharing conversation: half the time, you don't want to share either. You don't want to give up the good parking spot. You don't want to split the last slice. You ordered the large because you wanted the large. And now you're supposed to model generosity for a person who thinks sharing a banana means eating the banana and handing you the peel.

What the Research Actually Says (It's Complicated)

Here's where it gets interesting. There's a genuine debate in the parenting world about whether we should even force kids to share.

A study published in the journal Psychological Science by researchers at Cornell University found that when children were given the choice to share — rather than being compelled — they were actually more generous in future situations. The kids who were forced to hand over their toys? They shared less later on. Forced sharing taught compliance, not generosity.

Let that sink in. By making your kid share the dump truck, you might actually be creating a less generous adult. You're basically running a tiny authoritarian state that produces citizens who share only under duress. Which, come to think of it, tracks with most of human history.

The experts at the NIH break it down like this: true sharing requires something called Theory of Mind — the ability to understand that another person has desires and feelings different from your own. Toddlers are just barely beginning to develop this. An 18-month-old can't really grasp that another kid wants the truck. They just know they want the truck. And the truck is theirs. End of discussion. No appeals. The Supreme Court of Toddler has ruled.

By 24 months, kids start to get it — a little. They can share when there's no cost to them (giving away a cracker when they have ten crackers is doable) or when the other person explicitly asks. But asking a two-year-old to hand over the one toy they're actively playing with? You're essentially asking them to donate a kidney.

The Turn-Taking Hack

So if forced sharing backfires and toddlers can't truly share yet, what do you actually do when your kid is clutching a toy at the playground like it's the last helicopter out of Saigon?

The answer, according to basically every child development expert on the planet: turn-taking.

"Taking turns" is something toddler brains can actually process. It's concrete. It's sequential. It has rules they can follow. "Sharing" is this vague, abstract concept — am I giving it away forever? Will I get it back? What even IS this? But "you play with it, then I play with it" — that's a system. Toddlers love systems. They also love destroying systems, but they love having them first.

Here's how it works in practice:

At home: Pick up a ball. Say "my turn." Roll it around for a second. Then hand it over. "Your turn." Do this approximately 4,000 times. Eventually, the kid starts doing it back. This is not exciting content. It is not Instagram-worthy. It is the slow, boring work of building a human who doesn't hoard all the crayons at preschool.

At the playground: When another kid wants a turn on the swing, instead of demanding your kid hop off immediately, try: "Okay, you can have five more pushes, and then it's Aiden's turn." This respects your kid's current claim, establishes a timeline, and gives them a sense of control. Will they melt down when the five pushes are up? Possibly. Probably. But at least you gave them the framework.

The "Special Toy" Protocol: Before a playdate, let your kid pick two or three toys that are off limits — toys they don't have to share. Put those away. Everything else is fair game. This teaches them that their most valued possessions are safe, which actually makes them more willing to share the rest. Counterintuitive? Sure. But toddler psychology is basically a series of counterintuitive discoveries wrapped in a tantrum.

The Hypocrisy Problem

Can we talk about the elephant in the room? The one with a dad bod and a hidden stash of Oreos?

We expect our kids to share freely, graciously, without complaint. Meanwhile:

  • You have a "your side, my side" arrangement with the bed
  • You eat the last cookie and throw away the package so nobody knows
  • You have a phone charger that's "yours" and heaven help anyone who moves it
  • You once considered labeling your leftovers in the fridge

We're out here teaching sharing like we're monks who've renounced worldly possessions, when in reality we're just bigger, more sophisticated hoarders. We've just learned to disguise it as "organization" and "personal boundaries."

My buddy Dave told me his two-year-old son saw him refuse to let anyone change the TV channel during a football game and later used the exact same body language — arm extended, palm out, head shake — to block another kid from touching his toy truck. Dave looked at his wife. His wife looked at Dave. Nobody said anything. The defendant's own behavior was entered into evidence.

Kids don't learn sharing from lectures. They learn it from watching you hand your buddy a beer without being asked. From seeing you offer the last piece of pizza to your partner even though you definitely wanted it. From noticing that you let the old lady go ahead of you in the grocery line even when you were in a hurry.

They're absorbing everything. Which is terrifying, because they're also absorbing the time you pretended not to see your neighbor so you wouldn't have to make small talk.

The Ages and Stages Cheat Sheet

Because I know you want something practical, here's what to actually expect, age by age:

12–18 months: Your kid might "offer" you a toy or a piece of food. Don't get too excited. Research suggests this is mostly a social game — they want to see your reaction. They will absolutely take it back. This is not sharing. This is a toddler sting operation.

18–24 months: "MINE" becomes the national anthem. Your kid is figuring out ownership. This is normal and necessary. They might share if there's no cost to them (handing you a cracker from a full bag) but asking them to give up something they're using is a war crime in their eyes.

2–3 years: Turn-taking starts to click, especially with coaching. They can wait (briefly) if they know their turn is coming. Parallel play is still the dominant mode — kids this age play next to each other, not with each other. They don't need to share as much as we think.

3–4 years: This is where the magic starts. Theory of Mind develops. They begin to understand that other kids have feelings. Fair sharing — actually dividing things equally — starts showing up. The Society for Research in Child Development found that fair sharing behavior is linked to counting skills. So all those times you counted blocks together? That was sharing training in disguise.

4–5 years: Your kid can now share voluntarily, understand fairness, and even feel empathy about another kid's disappointment. They're still not going to be thrilled about it. But they can do it. Congratulations, you've raised a functioning member of society. Well, this one part of society. The other parts are still pending.

The Long Game

Here's the thing that got me through the worst playground standoffs, the most humiliating birthday party incidents, and the time my daughter told another kid "NO. MY DADDY." when the kid tried to sit on my lap at story time:

This is a long game.

You are not going to teach sharing in an afternoon. You're not going to teach it in a month. You're building neural pathways, one agonizing turn-taking exercise at a time. Every time you model generosity, every time you narrate the process ("I'm going to share my apple with you because I saw you were hungry"), every time you resist the urge to force a toy out of your kid's white-knuckle grip — you're laying bricks.

And one day — not today, probably not this month, maybe not even this year — your kid is going to walk up to another kid, hand them a toy, and say "here, you can play with this."

And you're going to stand there, blinking, wondering if you hallucinated it.

You didn't.

You just did the slow, invisible, deeply unglamorous work of raising a person who gives a damn about other people.

That's the gig.

The Real Truth

You know what finally made sharing click for me — as a concept, as a dad, as a guy who still hides the good ice cream behind the frozen vegetables?

It's not about the stuff. It was never about the stuff.

When my daughter "shares" her half-licked lollipop with me, she's not dividing resources. She's saying you matter to me. When she insists on giving her friend the "good" sticker, she's practicing a kind of love. Clumsy, sticky, developmentally incomplete love — but love.

And when I share my fries with her — willingly, not because she already stole them — I'm saying the same thing.

So no, you're not going to win the sharing wars overnight. Your kid is going to scream "MINE" approximately six thousand more times before kindergarten. You're going to stand on playgrounds apologizing to strangers. You're going to wonder if you're raising a tiny dictator with no concept of the social contract.

You are. That's literally what toddlers are. And it's your job to slowly, patiently, and probably while eating hidden snacks in the pantry, turn them into something better.

Welcome to the sharing front. Negotiations have barely begun. 🫡
